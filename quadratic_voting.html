Quadratic Voting

Quadratic voting is a decision-making method for groups. In this post, I will explain how it works and its pros and cons.

Assumptions

  • There are enough issues, and each issue is sufficiently inconsequential such that voters have a quasi-linear "continuation value" for retaining voice credits for future issues
  • Voice credits are distributed fairly
  • In deciding how many votes to purchase, each voter weighs the marginal cost of an additional vote against the perceived chance the vote will be pivotal in swinging the election
  • Voters agree on the marginal pivotality p

How It Works

Let's say voters are voting on an issue, and they're deciding between X and Y. Each voter has a certain number of "voice credits".

Each voter chooses some number of votes vi. A positive number means they want X, and a negative number means they want Y. A larger number, in terms of absolute value, indicates a stronger preference.

If the sum of all vi is positive, X wins. Otherwise, Y wins.

Each voter pays a cost according the following formula:

voice credits = c(vi)

c is a function that takes in a number of votes and returns the number of voice credits. Also, c must be differentiable, convex, even, and strictly increasing in |vi|.

Remember we have assumed that voters agree on the marginal pivotality p. Therefore, a rational voter will choose vi to maximize 2pviui - c(vi). Given the assumptions that voice credits are distributed fairly, society wishes to implement A when the sum of all ui >= 0. We say a vote pricing rule is robustly optimal if each voter i so that the sum of all vi has the same sign as the sum of all ui.

The Vote Pricing Rule

We have had all this talk about a theoretical vote pricing rule. We have also defined what it would look like for a vote pricing rule to be optimal. So what is the optimal vote pricing rule?

It is actually very simple.

voice credits = (number of votes)2

Vote pricing examples

Votes Voice credits
0 0
1 1
2 4
3 9
4 16

Proving Optimality

We have claimed that (number of votes)2 is the optimal vote pricing rule. How do we prove that?

The central idea is that quadratic functions are the only ones with linear derivatives. Therefore, a voter buying votes equates her marginal benefit and cost at a number of votes proportional to her value.
